About

I grew up in the 1980s and '90s and experienced, first-hand, the transition from analog to digital technologies. I remember having fun hooking up VCRs and CRT TVs, recording TV shows, sometimes doing live editing by pausing during the commercials(and then, hopefully remembering to unpause as soon as the program resumed). We also had a nice "hi-fi" system with separate phonograph, cassette, and receiver/amp devices. I enjoyed figuring out how the different machines connected with each other, and what all the many buttons and dials did. It was frustrating when I had trouble getting the big speakers to play the sound from the movie, or when I was on channel 4, trying to watch a VHS tape, but the switch in the back was set to channel 3. However, the sweet satisfaction that came when I was able to solve the problem and the system was operating as it was designed to do - that feeling made it all worth while. Being able to watch or listen to entertaining programs is a reward in itself, but I might have enjoyed the secondary reward of electronic engineering practice more than the entertainment.

Around 1992, we got our first PC, which came with MS-DOS. I played games on 5¼ and 3½ inch floppy discs(my favorite being Space Quest III). Dial-up internet was the only option at the time. We used that method to connect to the World Wide Web for many years. Click here to reminisce with me:

I remember in 1995, when Windows95 was unveiled to the world. In my recollection, the hype over this new OS was more than any since, including all of the "i" products(you know, pods, phones and pads, etc.) Apple had a memorable Super-Bowl commercial(which, for the record, I didn't see it upon first airing), and subsequent Apple and Microsoft OSs have much improved, along with the hardware, but the hype from Bill Gates, back in '95, supported by the news industry of the day - I have not seen its equal.

I remember taking a keyboarding class in high school(or was it middle school?). My memory's a little fuzzy on the equipment, but I think we used electronic typewriters that had a rudimentary digital LCD display that could fix typos if you spotted them soon enough. In any case, they were a form of typewriter, not a PC. We also had PCs in school. Yes, I remember Oregon Trail, but I didn't get to play it that much.

As a young man, most of my time on the PC consisted of playing video games. I also discovered chat rooms.

I generally don't like using labels because they are often inaccurate. But, given the opportunity to brand myself in order to prevent being prejudicially placed in a less desirable and less accurate group, I will declare that I identify with a lesser-known cohort termed "Xennials". This link from Forbes.com gives an excellent description of this newly coined "micro-generation" that straddled the shift from analog to digital in the 1980s and '90s. It has been defined as those born between 1977 and 1983, of which I am smack-dab in the middle. I remember well VCRs, cassette tapes and the like, but I was also young enough to easily adapt to the digital age with CDs, DVDs, PCs, and the modern smart devices. Because of that pre-digital experience, I don't have as much of a dependence on things like social media or digital maps. I like to use technology when it is useful, but I see it as a hinderance when overused, and I very much appreciate the value of "unplugging" and 'getting away' from the machines.

On a more distinctive level, I am from Texas, born and raised, where I enjoyed living in the rural suburbs most of that time. Church, including the one I was brought up in, and the one I converted to, has always been a major part of my life. After high school, I attended junior colleges in two different time periods, mixed with a variety of jobs and wondering about where my path would lead. Eventually, I made it back to college for the 3rd time (at least) and made it stick. In 2008, Christie, whom I met in church, was convinced to marry me :), and we tied the knot in the Seattle Washington Temple of our church. My first child, Lucy, was born at the end of my junior year in college. In 2010 I graduated from the University of North Texas with a B.A. in Mathematics. After my first year of teaching high school math, I moved here to Arizona 7 years ago with my pregnant(again) wife. Since then, we’ve been blessed with 3 more children: Lydia, Jacob, and then Charlotte.

The bouncing around from one job to the next continued here in Arizona, when on a fateful day on our way back from a family reunion in Montana, my wife saw an ad for the University of Arizona’s coding bootcamp. After some consideration, we decided to do it. I am very satisfied in the direction this class is leading me in. Part of me likes to tell myself that “I should have done this years ago”, but the more reasonable part of me says “yes, but good thing you’re doing it now”.

I look forward to using the skills, experience, and relationships I am building to get a new job. I have a number of things that, through the years, I have wanted to create, but did not have the know-how. I believe I can contribute much to our society and hopefully earn a decent living doing so.
